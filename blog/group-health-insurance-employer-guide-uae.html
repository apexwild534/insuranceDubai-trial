The legal framework — what UAE law actually requires of employers

There are three overlapping legal frameworks that govern employer health insurance obligations in the UAE. Understanding which applies to your employees is the first step.

1. Dubai — DHA (Dubai Health Authority)

Dubai Law No. 11 of 2013 requires all employers operating in Dubai to provide health insurance for every employee working in the emirate. This includes UAE nationals, expatriate employees, part-time staff and — importantly — domestic workers whose employer is based in Dubai. The law has been fully enforced since 2016. DHA compliance is checked at the point of visa processing through the Immigration Department; without a valid insurance certificate linked to the employee's Emirates ID, their residence visa will not be issued or renewed.

Dubai minimum requirement: Any DHA-approved health insurance plan. There is no single minimum plan for Dubai (unlike the MOHRE national mandate), but the plan must be issued by a DHA-licensed insurer and meet minimum benefit standards set by DHA.

2. Abu Dhabi — DOH (Department of Health)

Abu Dhabi has the most comprehensive employer obligations in the UAE. Under DOH regulations in force since 2006, employers must provide health insurance not only for employees but also for their direct dependents — spouse and up to three children under 18. This is unique to Abu Dhabi and frequently catches employers who relocate staff from Dubai to Abu Dhabi without updating their insurance arrangements. DOH compliance is enforced strictly; fines for non-compliance can reach AED 5,000 per uninsured individual per violation.

3. Federal MOHRE mandate — effective January 2025

The most recent and widest-reaching framework is the federal mandate administered by the Ministry of Human Resources and Emiratisation (MOHRE). From 1 January 2025, all private sector employers across the UAE — not just Dubai and Abu Dhabi — must provide a minimum health insurance plan for employees earning AED 4,000 or less per month. The designated plan is the Essential Benefits Plan (commonly called the MOHRE Basic Plan), priced at AED 320 per year per employee.

Important: The MOHRE mandate is a federal minimum floor, not a ceiling. In Dubai and Abu Dhabi, employers must still meet the higher DHA and DOH standards above. The MOHRE mandate brings the Northern Emirates (Sharjah, Ajman, RAK, UAQ, Fujairah) into the mandatory framework for the first time.

Group size and how it affects your options

The number of employees you are insuring directly affects which insurers will quote you and what rates are available:

  • 1–4 employees: Very small groups. Most major insurers require a minimum of 3–5 lives for group pricing. Below this threshold, individual plans for each employee may be the only option — or some insurers offer micro-group products. Expect to pay closer to individual rates.
  • 5–20 employees: Small group. Most insurers will quote, but underwriting is often on a case-by-case basis and rates are closer to individual than large group. Pre-existing conditions in this size group can significantly affect the overall premium.
  • 21–100 employees: Standard SME group. Full group underwriting applies. You can negotiate network tiers, co-payment structures, and get meaningful competition between 4–6 insurers.
  • 100+ employees: Large group. Experience-rated pricing based on your company's actual claims history. More negotiating power, more tailored plan design, and access to self-insurance or partial self-insurance structures.

Setting up a group health insurance plan — the process

1

Prepare your employee census

You need: full name, date of birth, nationality, Emirates ID number, salary band, and any known pre-existing conditions for each employee to be covered. Accuracy matters — incorrect DOB or Emirates ID causes delays at visa renewal.

2

Determine your plan requirements

Decide on your preferred plan tier, network (DHA clinics only vs private hospitals), co-payment structure, annual limit, and whether dependents are included. Your broker will translate these into a brief for insurers.

3

Obtain quotes from multiple insurers

A broker with multi-insurer access will go to market simultaneously. For groups under 20, expect 5–10 business days for quotes. For groups over 50, allow 10–15 business days as insurers may request additional health information.

4

Review policy wording, not just premium

The cheapest quote is not always the right choice. Review co-payment levels, annual limits per employee, maternity cover terms, pre-existing condition handling, and mid-year addition/deletion process before signing.

5

Issue insurance cards and communicate to employees

Employees need their insurance card and policy schedule before they can use the plan. Make sure each employee understands their co-payment, their network, and how to find in-network facilities. Poor communication is the main reason for avoidable claims rejections.

Mid-year additions and deletions — managing a live group policy

One of the most common pain points for HR teams is managing the group policy throughout the year as headcount changes. Here is how the process works:

  • New hires: Must be added to the policy within 30 days of their start date in most plans. Adding after 30 days may trigger underwriting review or a pre-existing condition exclusion period. Always notify your broker on the day of joining.
  • Departing employees: Must be removed from the policy on their last day of employment. Failure to remove ex-employees can create liability issues and inflated renewal premiums. Most insurers issue a pro-rata refund for unused cover.
  • Maternity additions: Newborns must typically be added within 30–60 days of birth to maintain automatic newborn cover. Notify your insurer immediately after any employee's baby is born if dependents are covered under your group plan.
  • Salary band changes: If an employee moves from under AED 4,000 to over AED 4,000 per month (or vice versa), their required cover tier changes. Make sure your policy allows mid-year upgrades without penalty.

Employer compliance checklist — 2026

✅ UAE Employer Health Insurance Compliance Checklist

  • All employees — including part-time and domestic workers — are enrolled in a DHA/DOH/MOHRE-approved health insurance plan
  • Employees earning ≤AED 4,000/month are enrolled in MOHRE Basic or equivalent before visa renewal date
  • Abu Dhabi-based employees have dependent cover (spouse + up to 3 children under 18) arranged
  • Insurance certificates for all employees are linked to their Emirates ID in the DHA/ICA systems
  • New hires are added to the policy within 30 days of joining
  • Departed employees are removed from the policy within 30 days of leaving
  • Group policy renewal date is tracked and renewal process starts at least 60 days in advance
  • Employees have received their insurance cards and understand how to use their plan
  • HR team knows the claims process and has the insurer's helpline and TPA details
  • Broker relationship is in place for mid-year changes, claims disputes and renewal negotiations

Frequently asked questions for UAE employers

Do I have to insure part-time employees?

Yes, in Dubai. The DHA law applies to all employees regardless of working hours, including part-time and domestic workers sponsored by a company. Part-time workers sponsored by an individual (domestic staff) are covered under the individual sponsor's obligation. The MOHRE federal mandate also applies to part-time employees on a UAE work permit, regardless of hours worked.

Can employees contribute to the cost of their own health insurance?

UAE law does not specify who must pay the premium — only that the employer must arrange the cover. In practice, most employers pay 100% of the basic employee plan as a standard benefit. Employers can ask employees to contribute to the cost of enhanced cover or dependent cover upgrades above the basic plan, though this should be clearly set out in the employment contract.

What happens if an employee's visa lapses and they continue working?

If an employee's visa lapses, their insurance certificate is also technically invalid. This creates a compliance gap for both the employee and the employer. Most insurers will continue to honour claims during a short renewal processing period if the renewal is actively in progress, but this is not guaranteed. Always process visa renewals before they lapse to maintain continuous insurance compliance.

How much does group health insurance cost per employee in the UAE?

Group health insurance costs in the UAE range from AED 320 per employee per year (MOHRE Basic plan) to AED 15,000+ per employee for premium international plans. The most common range for a standard professional group plan (mid-tier enhanced) in Dubai is AED 2,000–4,500 per employee per year, depending on the group's age profile, size, and claims history.
