Is health insurance mandatory for Dubai freelancers?

Yes — unambiguously. Under Dubai Law No. 11 of 2013 (the Dubai Health Insurance Law), all Dubai residents are required to have valid health insurance. This applies whether you are employed by a company, self-employed, freelance, or retired. The DHA (Dubai Health Authority) enforces compliance at the visa issuance and renewal stage. If you cannot present a valid insurance certificate from a DHA-approved insurer, your Dubai visa will not be issued or renewed.

The law was originally phased in by employer size, but by 2016 it covered all categories of residents including self-sponsored individuals. There are no exceptions for freelancers, part-time workers, or low-income earners. The minimum acceptable plan is the MOHRE Basic (Enhanced) plan, which costs AED 320 per year — but as we will explain below, that plan is often inadequate for a working freelancer's actual needs.

Key distinction: If you hold a freelance permit issued through a free zone (like Dubai Media City, Dubai Internet City, or TECOM), the free zone itself does not arrange your health insurance — you are responsible for purchasing it directly, the same as any self-sponsored resident.

The MOHRE Basic Plan — what freelancers need to know

The MOHRE Basic Plan (officially the Essential Benefits Plan) costs AED 320 per year and is the minimum compliant plan under UAE regulations. It was designed primarily for low-income workers earning under AED 4,000 per month and is only available through a small number of approved insurers (currently: Daman, Oman Insurance / Orient, and a few others).

For freelancers, the MOHRE Basic plan has significant limitations you should understand before choosing it:

  • Very limited outpatient network: You can only visit DHA clinics and a small list of approved facilities. Popular private hospitals (Medcare, Mediclinic, American Hospital) are typically not included.
  • AED 150,000 annual limit: While this sounds sufficient, a single major procedure — cardiac surgery, cancer treatment, complex delivery — can exceed this. Plans with higher limits are strongly recommended for freelancers without employer backup.
  • No dental or optical cover at the basic tier
  • Pre-existing condition waiting period: 6 months for pre-existing conditions, same as higher plans
  • No reimbursement claims easily available: You must use the in-network facilities; out-of-network reimbursement is not a standard feature

The MOHRE Basic plan is compliant for visa purposes and is a reasonable choice if you are a healthy young freelancer with low healthcare usage and a tight budget. However, most working freelancers — especially those over 35, with dependents, or with any health conditions — will find it inadequate.

Pre-existing conditions — the biggest issue for freelancers

This is the area where freelancers face the most difficulty compared to employees. When you join a company's group health plan, pre-existing conditions are often covered from day one because the insurer prices the risk across the group. When you buy an individual plan as a freelancer, insurers assess your health individually — and pre-existing conditions are treated very differently.

Under UAE regulations, insurers cannot permanently exclude pre-existing conditions from individual plans. However they can:

  • Apply a 6-month waiting period before covering treatment related to a pre-existing condition
  • Apply a premium loading (additional charge) of 20–50% or more for higher-risk applicants
  • Require a medical examination or specialist report before issuing cover
  • In some cases, decline to offer individual cover entirely — though this is uncommon for well-managed conditions

Common conditions that affect individual underwriting: hypertension, diabetes (Type 1 or 2), thyroid disorders, asthma, depression or anxiety, obesity (BMI over 35), and any history of cancer or cardiac events. If you have any of these, be upfront with your broker — non-disclosure discovered at claim time can result in a rejected claim, regardless of your premium payments.

Freelancer tip: If you are leaving a salaried job with group cover and moving to freelance, buy your new individual plan before your group cover lapses. You may be able to get a continuity clause that carries over your existing cover period for pre-existing conditions, reducing or eliminating the waiting period.

Can freelancers add family members to their health insurance?

Yes. Self-sponsored Dubai residents can purchase individual policies for each family member, or in some cases a family floater plan that covers multiple members under a single policy. There is no requirement for an employer to be involved in insuring your dependents. The options are:

  • Separate individual policies for each family member: Most common and most flexible. Each member is underwritten separately, so a pre-existing condition in one member does not affect others' premiums.
  • Family plans (limited availability in UAE): Some insurers offer family-rate plans that bundle cover for two adults and up to three children at a slightly discounted rate versus buying individual policies separately. GIG Gulf and Bupa offer this.
  • Adding dependents to your policy as riders: Less common, but some insurers allow you to add a spouse or child to your individual policy. This is typically simpler administratively but less flexible at renewal.

Tax deductibility of health insurance for freelancers

The UAE does not currently have personal income tax, so there is no tax deduction mechanism for individual health insurance premiums paid by self-employed individuals. If you operate through a UAE company structure (LLC or free zone company), health insurance premiums paid by the company for employees — including yourself as an employee-director — are a deductible business expense under UAE Corporate Tax rules that came into effect in June 2023. Self-employed individuals without a company structure have no equivalent deduction mechanism.

Frequently asked questions

Can I get health insurance without a UAE visa?

Most UAE health insurers require a valid Emirates ID or UAE residence visa to issue a policy. If you are in the process of obtaining your freelance permit and visa, your free zone or mainland authority will usually issue a temporary permit that allows you to purchase insurance. Speak to your broker about interim options during the visa application period.

What happens to my health insurance if I cancel my freelance permit?

Your individual health insurance policy is tied to your visa status. If you cancel your freelance permit and leave the UAE, your policy can be cancelled (with a pro-rata refund in most cases) or, if you are transitioning to a new visa type, transferred to the new status. Never let your visa and insurance lapse simultaneously — a gap in cover can affect future applications.

Is health insurance cheaper if I buy through my free zone?

Some free zones have negotiated group rates with specific insurers (DMCC and Dubai Internet City, for example, have preferred insurer lists). If you are a member of such a free zone, check whether a group rate is available — it can be 15–25% cheaper than buying individually. However, group plans through free zones are usually basic tier and may have restricted networks. Compare carefully before assuming the free zone option is better value.

Can I claim health insurance as a business expense?

If you operate through a UAE registered company (LLC or free zone entity), health insurance premiums paid by the company for employees (including yourself as a director/employee) are deductible business expenses under UAE Corporate Tax regulations. Sole proprietors without a formal company structure cannot deduct personal health insurance premiums. Speak to a UAE tax adviser for guidance on your specific structure.
