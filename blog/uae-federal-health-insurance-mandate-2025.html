UAE Federal Health Insurance Mandate 2025 — Full Guide (Updated 29 April 2026)

By the InsuranceDubai.com editorial team · Updated 29 April 2026 · 12 min read

On 1 January 2025, the United Arab Emirates rolled out one of the most significant changes to its healthcare system in two decades: a federal mandate making health insurance compulsory for every private-sector employee and domestic worker across the country. Sixteen months later — as of late April 2026 — the policy is fully active, the AED 320 federal Basic Health Insurance Scheme has expanded to more than 380 healthcare providers, and most competitor pages on the open web still have not been updated. This guide brings together every piece of regulation, premium, co-payment cap and fine published by the Ministry of Health and Prevention (MOHAP), the Ministry of Human Resources and Emiratisation (MOHRE), the Department of Health Abu Dhabi (DOH) and the Dubai Health Authority (DHA), in one place.

What changed on 1 January 2025

Until the end of 2024, mandatory health insurance in the UAE was a patchwork. Abu Dhabi had required employer-funded health cover since 2006 under DOH (formerly HAAD) regulations. Dubai had followed in 2014 under the DHA's Mandatory Health Insurance scheme. The other five emirates — Sharjah, Ajman, Ras Al Khaimah, Fujairah and Umm Al Quwain — had no equivalent law, leaving roughly two million workers uninsured by their employers.

Federal Decree-Law No. 33 of 2021, supplemented by Cabinet Decision No. 11 of 2024, fixed that gap. From 1 January 2025:

  • Health insurance is mandatory across all seven emirates.
  • Employers are liable for 100% of the premium; employees cannot be asked to contribute.
  • Coverage must be active before or on the employee's first working day and run continuously throughout employment.
  • No new residency visa or work permit can be issued or renewed without a linked policy — the rule is enforced in real time through the MOHRE-ICP Unified Health Insurance Gateway.
  • Domestic workers (Tadbeer category) are now also covered.

The federal Basic Health Insurance Scheme — administered by Dubai Insurance, Daman, Sukoon, GIG Gulf, Liva, NAS, Nextcare and other accredited carriers — anchors the new minimum at a base premium of AED 320 per person per year.

The AED 320 MOHRE Basic Plan in detail

Most coverage gaps that the mandate addressed were in the Northern Emirates, so the federal Basic Plan was deliberately designed to be inexpensive enough for SMEs to afford and rich enough to be medically meaningful. Here is what the AED 320 product actually buys, in 2026:

Three small details matter here, because they routinely catch HR managers off guard:

  • 2-year validity. The policy is sold as a 24-month product. If the work permit is cancelled in the first year, the second-year premium is refundable to the employer.
  • No chronic-illness waiting period. Diabetes, hypertension and similar conditions are covered from day one — a major upgrade over the older Dubai Essential Benefits Plan (EBP).
  • Age cap. The AED 320 rate applies to ages 1 to 64. Workers aged 65+ require an enhanced plan with medical disclosure.

Who is covered, who is exempt

The mandate is expansive but not absolute. UAE nationals are covered by separate government schemes (Thiqa in Abu Dhabi, Saada in Dubai, ENAYA federally) and so do not need a private policy. Employees whose work permits were issued before 1 January 2025 are grandfathered until their next renewal, after which insurance becomes mandatory. The federal scheme also does not yet require dependent cover by default — although Abu Dhabi continues to mandate it for spouses and up to three children under 18, and Dubai is expected to align in 2027.

If you employ anyone in the following groups, you must arrange cover:

  • Private-sector employees on full-time, part-time or limited contracts.
  • Domestic workers under MOHRE's Tadbeer system (housemaids, drivers, cooks, gardeners, nannies).
  • Free-zone workers — including DIFC, ADGM, JAFZA, DMCC and Hamriyah Free Zone employees.
  • Workers on probation. Cover must start on the first day, not after probation ends.

How much it really costs employers

The headline AED 320 number is misleading on its own. After VAT and ICP fees, the all-in cost per employee for a Basic Plan currently sits at around AED 361 per year. Group rates negotiated through an aggregator like InsuranceDubai.com typically come in 10–15% below retail. For a 50-person SME in Sharjah or Ajman, total annual compliance spend on the AED 320 plan therefore lands around AED 15,500 – AED 17,000, plus dependents if the company chooses to extend cover.

Where employers want richer cover — direct-billing networks, dental, optical, maternity riders, mental-health benefits, evacuation, or higher annual limits — premiums scale up to AED 2,000–4,000 for an enhanced individual plan, and AED 5,000–25,000+ for senior staff and family plans. The good news: under the new scheme, even the cheapest legal product gives employees full coverage for in-patient surgery up to AED 50,000 a year, which was not previously the case in the Northern Emirates.

Fines and enforcement in 2026

Compliance is enforced by MOHRE jointly with the Federal Authority for Identity, Citizenship, Customs and Ports (ICP). The system is live and machine-checked at visa renewal:

  • AED 500 to AED 2,500 per employee per month for missing or lapsed cover.
  • AED 150,000 first-offence administrative fine on the company.
  • AED 500,000 for repeat offences, plus visa-issuance blocks and possible business-licence suspension.
  • Employers who attempt to deduct premiums from employee wages face an additional fine and immediate cancellation of the policy.
"The unified gateway means non-compliance no longer slips through. If a worker's visa is up for renewal and the gateway shows no policy, the renewal cannot proceed — full stop." — UAE insurance market briefing, Q1 2026.

What changed between January 2025 and April 2026

The most significant operational updates over the first 16 months of the mandate were:

  • Network expansion to 380+ providers. Including most JCI-accredited hospitals across all five Northern Emirates and a meaningful pharmacy network.
  • Direct-billing rollout. Sukoon, GIG Gulf, Liva, NAS and Nextcare moved AED 320 plan members from claim-and-reimburse to direct billing in late 2025.
  • MOHRE-ICP single-window subscription. Employers can now register, pay and receive a digital insurance card in a single online journey.
  • Domestic-worker uptake. By March 2026, more than 800,000 domestic workers had been onboarded onto the federal scheme — a figure that was negligible at launch.
  • Refund of unused second-year premiums. Originally manual, this is now automated when an employer cancels a permit before the 12-month mark.

How the mandate interacts with ILOE and other cover

Many residents conflate the federal health-insurance mandate with the ILOE scheme. They are entirely separate: ILOE is unemployment cover; the AED 320 plan is medical cover. However, both are MOHRE-administered and both are mandatory, so for a typical private-sector worker the legal compliance picture in 2026 is:

  • Federal Basic Health Insurance Plan — paid by the employer.
  • ILOE Insurance Scheme — paid by the employee (AED 5 or AED 10 per month).
  • Optional: enhanced health, life, mortgage, and private job loss insurance UAE top-up.

Practical checklist for HR managers

If you run an SME or HR function in any of the Northern Emirates, the following 8-point checklist captures everything you need to be compliant in 2026:

  • Audit every employee whose work permit was issued before 1 January 2025 — they need cover at next renewal.
  • Confirm coverage is in force before day one. Do not allow grace days.
  • Subscribe through an accredited insurer registered on the MOHRE-ICP Unified Health Insurance Gateway.
  • Use direct billing where available to minimise out-of-pocket cost for employees.
  • Track expiry dates for the 2-year cycle and request second-year refunds for cancelled permits.
  • Publish a one-page summary of the plan to staff (network, co-pays, exclusions).
  • For senior or older staff, upgrade above AED 320 — Sukoon, GIG Gulf, Liva and Nextcare have ready-built tiered packages.
  • Pair with the mandatory ILOE scheme and consider a private job loss insurance UAE rider for senior staff.

Looking ahead — 2026 and beyond

Two policy directions are widely expected. First, the federal Cabinet has signalled that dependent cover may become mandatory across all emirates from 2027, harmonising the Northern Emirates with the Abu Dhabi standard. Second, parametric and digital-first products are growing fast: insurers including Sukoon and Liva have launched in-app claim filing with same-day payout for outpatient visits, narrowing the convenience gap with regional fintechs.

For employers, the headline is simple: the era of unevenly-mandated UAE health cover is over. The compliance bar is now uniform, the Basic Plan is competitively priced, and the cost of getting it wrong is significantly higher than the cost of getting it right.
